# Timeline of Early Buddhism: Major Events and Written Texts

_September 15, 2025_

## 6th-5th Century BCE
**c. 563-483 BCE** - Life of Siddhartha Gautama (the Buddha)
- Traditional dates vary by tradition (Theravada: 563-483 BCE; some scholars: 490-410 BCE)
- Oral transmission of teachings begins immediately after Buddha's death

**c. 483 BCE** - First Buddhist Council at Rajagaha
- Ananda recites the Suttas (discourses)
- Upali recites the Vinaya (monastic rules)
- All teachings preserved orally in various Indian languages

## 4th-3rd Century BCE
**c. 383 BCE** - Second Buddhist Council at Vesali
- Dispute over monastic rules leads to first major schism
- Split between Mahasanghikas and Sthaviravadins
- Continued oral transmission

**c. 272-232 BCE** - Reign of Emperor Ashoka
- Major patron of Buddhism; facilitates spread across Asia
- Edicts carved in stone (earliest written Buddhist materials, though not canonical texts)

**c. 250 BCE** - Third Buddhist Council at Pataliputra (disputed historicity)
- Allegedly organized by Ashoka
- Traditional Theravada account of doctrinal purification

## 2nd Century BCE - 1st Century CE
**c. 100 BCE** - Pali Canon first written down in Sri Lanka
- **MAJOR MILESTONE**: End of purely oral tradition for Theravada Buddhism
- Written in Pali on palm leaves during reign of King Vattagamani Abhaya
- Includes Vinaya Pitaka, Sutta Pitaka, and Abhidhamma Pitaka
- Location: Aluvihara, Sri Lanka

**c. 100-50 BCE** - Gandhara manuscripts (earliest known Buddhist manuscripts)
- Written in Gandhari (Prakrit) using Kharoshthi script
- Found in modern-day Afghanistan/Pakistan
- Include various sutras and Vinaya texts

## 1st-2nd Century CE
**c. 50-150 CE** - Development of Mahayana literature
- Perfection of Wisdom (Prajnaparamita) sutras composed
- Initially oral, gradual transition to written form in Sanskrit

**c. 100 CE** - Fourth Buddhist Council (Kashmir)
- Organized by Kanishka I
- Sarvastivadin school produces commentaries
- Texts likely written in Sanskrit

## 2nd-5th Century CE
**c. 150-400 CE** - Major Sanskrit Buddhist texts written down
- Lotus Sutra (Saddharmapundarika)
- Vimalakirti Sutra
- Avatamsaka Sutra
- Lankavatara Sutra
- Various Prajnaparamita texts

**c. 200 CE** - Nagarjuna's Mulamadhyamakakarika
- Foundational Madhyamaka philosophy text in Sanskrit

**c. 300-400 CE** - Yogacara school texts
- Asanga's works
- Vasubandhu's Abhidharmakoshabhashya
- Written in Sanskrit

## Chinese Transmission

**c. 148 CE** - An Shigao arrives in China
- First systematic translation of Buddhist texts into Chinese
- Focuses on meditation and Abhidharma texts

**c. 179 CE** - Lokakshema's translations
- First Mahayana sutras translated into Chinese
- Including early Prajnaparamita literature

**c. 402-412 CE** - Kumarajiva's translation project
- **MAJOR MILESTONE**: Systematic translation of major Mahayana texts
- Lotus Sutra, Diamond Sutra, Vimalakirti Sutra translated into Chinese
- Established standard Chinese Buddhist terminology

**c. 399-414 CE** - Faxian's pilgrimage to India
- Brings back Sanskrit texts for translation

**c. 629-645 CE** - Xuanzang's pilgrimage to India
- **MAJOR MILESTONE**: Massive translation project upon return
- 1,335 fascicles of texts translated into Chinese
- Yogacara texts, Abhidharma literature

## Tibetan Transmission

**c. 650-700 CE** - Introduction of Buddhism to Tibet
- Songtsen Gampo's reign
- First Buddhist temples built

**c. 760s-770s CE** - Tibetan script adapted for Buddhist texts
- **MAJOR MILESTONE**: Beginning of systematic translation into Tibetan
- Padmasambhava and Shantarakshita active in Tibet

**c. 780s CE** - First Buddhist texts translated into Tibetan
- Primarily from Sanskrit originals
- Initial focus on Tantric literature

**c. 816-838 CE** - Systematic translation projects
- Reign of Ralpachen
- Standardization of translation terminology
- Many Mahayana sutras and Tantras translated

## Other Important Developments

**c. 400-600 CE** - Pali commentaries written down
- Buddhaghosa's Visuddhimagga (c. 430 CE)
- Comprehensive commentaries on Pali Canon
- Written in Sri Lanka

**c. 500-700 CE** - Theravada texts translated into Southeast Asian languages
- Burmese, Thai, Khmer, Lao translations begin
- Based on Pali originals

**c. 700-1000 CE** - Late Sanskrit Buddhist literature
- Tantric texts (Guhyasamaja, Chakrasamvara, etc.)
- Final major compositions in Indian Buddhism

## Key Observations

**Oral to Written Transition Timeline:**
1. **Pali (c. 100 BCE)** - First complete canonical collection written down
2. **Gandhari/Sanskrit (c. 50 BCE-200 CE)** - Gradual written recording
3. **Chinese (c. 150-700 CE)** - Major translation period
4. **Tibetan (c. 760-850 CE)** - Systematic translation from Sanskrit

**Geographic Spread of Written Traditions:**
- **Sri Lanka**: Pali preservation center
- **Central Asia**: Gandhari manuscripts, Silk Road transmission
- **India**: Sanskrit composition and preservation (until c. 1200 CE)
- **China**: Major translation hub for East Asian Buddhism
- **Tibet**: Final major translation project, preservation of late Indian texts

**Note on Dating**: Many dates, especially for the Buddha's life and early councils, remain subjects of scholarly debate. The timeline represents commonly accepted scholarly estimates while noting traditional dating where it differs significantly.
